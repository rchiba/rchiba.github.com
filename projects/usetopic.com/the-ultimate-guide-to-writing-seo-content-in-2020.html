All of these changes mean that the focus of SEO has fundamentally shifted from satisfying the search engine to satisfying the reader.

Quotes - Find out which experts your competitors are quoting as inspiration for who you can contact to do the same. Quotes from experts increase the trustworthiness of your content and can offer unique perspectives beyond your own.

Step 3: Write your draft


Now comes the most challenging (and fun) part. Turning your content outline into a first draft.

At this point, your goal should be to write content that‚Äôs better than the content you are competing with.

You want to find a unique way that will jump out to the reader and add even more value. Here are a couple of ways to create something unique:

  • Freshness - If you‚Äôre writing about a subject that changes quickly, then being more up-to-date than other content can give you a leg up.
  • Comprehensiveness - If all of the other content is relatively thin, you can focus on having the most comprehensive content.

  • Brand - Are you writing for an organization with a powerful brand? Piggyback on that power to stand out from the crowd.
  • Data - If you have access to data that no-one else has, you can create content no-one else can replicate.
  • Perspective - Maybe you have an opinion that differs from the mainstream. Or you have a unique point of view.
  • User Experience - If competing content is full of ads and popups, then there's an opportunity to create a cleaner and easier-to-use experience. You should pay attention to mobile responsiveness, page load speed, and the design of the layout. The less friction a user has to get to the information they need, the less likely they will bounce.

The key is to use the research you've done to create a piece of content that engages your audience more than your competitors.
